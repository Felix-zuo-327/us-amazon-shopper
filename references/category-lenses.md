# Category Lenses

When the product belongs to a specific category, overlay these focus modifiers on top of the active persona. The core shopping logic (trust, convenience, return risk) stays the same. The category lens adjusts **what the shopper pays attention to first**.

If the user does not specify a category, infer it from the product. If the category is ambiguous, say so and pick the closest fit.

## 1. Electronics & Tech

Primary anxiety: compatibility and counterfeit risk.

What this shopper checks first:
- "Will this work with my device?" — compatibility details are non-negotiable
- Spec sheets and comparison tables matter more than lifestyle photos
- Brand recognition weighs heavily because knockoff fear is real
- Warranty length, return window, and customer service responsiveness
- Whether the seller is the actual brand or a random third-party reseller

Specific trust breakers:
- Missing model compatibility list
- No mention of certifications (FCC, UL, MFi for Apple accessories)
- "Updated version" or "2024 model" without explaining what changed
- Product title stuffed with specs that contradict each other
- Reviews mentioning "stopped working after 2 months"

Price behavior:
- $15-30 accessories: impulse zone, brand matters less, star rating and review count dominate
- $50-150 mid-range: moderate research, YouTube cross-reference is common
- $150+ premium: heavy research, external review sites, warranty comparison, counterfeit concern peaks

Review scanning habits:
- Filters by "most recent" to catch quality-decline signals
- Searches review text for specific device names ("iPhone 15", "Samsung S24")
- Photo reviews showing internal components or real-world setup carry 10x weight
- 3-star reviews are the most informative: "it works but..."

## 2. Supplements & Vitamins

Primary anxiety: safety and efficacy trust.

What this shopper checks first:
- Third-party testing logos (USP, NSF, ConsumerLab) — their absence is a red flag
- Full ingredient list visibility, not just headline claims
- "Proprietary blend" triggers distrust: "What are you hiding?"
- Manufacturing location and GMP certification
- FDA disclaimer awareness: savvy buyers know supplements are not FDA-approved

Specific trust breakers:
- No third-party testing mentioned anywhere
- Vague claims like "boosts immunity" or "supports wellness" without specifics
- Dosage that seems too high or too low compared to known standards
- Reviews mentioning side effects, stomach issues, or "didn't do anything"
- Capsule size complaints (especially for older buyers)
- "Amazon's Choice" on a supplement from an unknown brand feels risky, not reassuring

Price behavior:
- Per-serving cost matters more than total price
- Subscribe & Save is the default purchase mode for repeat supplements
- Below $10 for a 90-day supply: "Is this real?"
- Above $40 for a single ingredient: "Am I paying for the brand?"

Review scanning habits:
- Searches for specific health outcomes mentioned in reviews
- Cross-references with health forums, Reddit, or ConsumerLab
- Watches for "I've been taking this for 6 months" long-term reviews
- Vine reviews on supplements are treated with more skepticism than other categories

## 3. Beauty & Personal Care

Primary anxiety: personal suitability and ingredient safety.

What this shopper checks first:
- Ingredient list and whether "clean beauty" claims are substantiated
- Skin type or hair type compatibility ("Will this work for MY skin?")
- Texture, scent, and feel described in reviews (photos help but can't convey everything)
- Packaging aesthetics and perceived quality
- Whether the product is sold by the actual brand (counterfeit skincare is a real fear)

Specific trust breakers:
- No ingredient list visible in images or bullets
- Shade or color that looks different in every photo
- Reviews saying "smells weird" or "broke me out"
- Packaging that arrives damaged or looks different from listing photos
- "Cruelty-free" or "organic" claims without recognized certifications (Leaping Bunny, USDA Organic)
- Products that claim to treat medical conditions (acne, eczema) without appropriate disclaimers

Price behavior:
- Wide acceptable range: $8 drugstore dupe to $60 prestige, context-dependent
- Under $12: "Let me try it, worst case I return it"
- $25-45: moderate research, ingredient comparison
- $60+: extensive research, influencer reviews, sample/trial size preference
- Subscription makes sense for daily-use items (moisturizer, cleanser, SPF)

Review scanning habits:
- Photo reviews weighted extremely heavily (real skin, real hair results)
- Filters by skin type mentions in review text
- Watches for "after 2 weeks" and "after 1 month" follow-up reviews
- Influencer mentions can help OR hurt (sponsored content skepticism)
- Patch test / allergy mentions taken very seriously

## 4. Baby & Kids Products

Primary anxiety: safety above everything.

What this shopper checks first:
- Safety certifications (CPSC, ASTM, JPMA) — non-negotiable
- Age-appropriateness and choking hazard warnings
- Other parents' real-use reviews (especially for durability and safety)
- Assembly complexity and time required
- Material safety (BPA-free, phthalate-free, non-toxic finishes)
- Product recall history (informed parents check CPSC.gov)

Specific trust breakers:
- No safety certification mentioned
- Reviews mentioning sharp edges, loose parts, or tipping risk
- Assembly described as "nightmare" in multiple reviews
- Materials that smell chemical or feel cheap
- Age range that seems too broad ("ages 0-12" on a single toy)
- Missing or confusing size information (especially for car seats, cribs, clothing)

Price behavior:
- Parents pay more for proven safety and durability
- Under $15: gift or trial territory, lower scrutiny
- $30-80: core purchase zone, moderate research
- $100+: heavy research, brand loyalty kicks in (Graco, UPPAbaby, Halo)
- Emotional buying + rational safety checking creates unique tension: "I want the cute one but I need the safe one"

Review scanning habits:
- Other parents' opinions carry more weight than any marketing copy
- "My toddler..." reviews are gold
- Searches for specific safety incidents in review text
- Photo reviews showing real kids using the product are the strongest signal
- 1-star reviews about safety issues are taken as absolute warnings, even if only 1-2 exist

## 5. Home & Kitchen

Primary anxiety: "Will this actually fit and work in MY home?"

What this shopper checks first:
- Exact dimensions and whether they match real-world spaces (countertop, cabinet, shelf)
- Material and build quality signals
- Cleaning and maintenance difficulty
- Whether lifestyle photos show realistic home settings or staged magazine shots
- Assembly required? If yes, how bad?
- Storage — "Where does this go when I'm not using it?"

Specific trust breakers:
- No dimension information or only metric measurements
- Photos that look too perfect (3D renders instead of real product shots)
- Reviews mentioning "smaller than expected" or "cheap-feeling"
- "Hand wash only" on something that should be dishwasher safe
- Instructions described as confusing or missing
- Wobbling, tipping, or instability mentioned in reviews

Price behavior:
- $10-25: impulse-to-moderate, aesthetic appeal can drive the buy
- $25-75: core home purchase zone, research involved
- $75-200: comparison shopping, brand matters, durability expectations
- $200+: "This better last years" territory, warranty important
- Multi-pack logic common (storage containers, hangers, kitchen tools)

Review scanning habits:
- "After 6 months of use" reviews carry the most weight
- Photo reviews showing the product in real kitchens/homes
- Size comparison with common objects (soda can, book, hand)
- Cleaning ease is frequently the make-or-break detail in reviews
- Returns due to size mismatch are extremely common — any ambiguity kills confidence

## 6. Clothing & Shoes

Primary anxiety: fit and sizing.

What this shopper checks first:
- Sizing information: specific measurements (waist, inseam, bust) vs vague S/M/L
- Fabric content and feel (reviews that describe texture are essential)
- Color accuracy between listing photos and real life
- Fit descriptions in reviews: "runs small," "runs large," "true to size"
- Return ease — many shoppers plan to buy two sizes and return one

Specific trust breakers:
- Size chart with only S/M/L and no measurements
- Color in photos that looks different from image to image
- Models with no height/weight reference
- Reviews split on sizing ("runs small" AND "runs big" = confusing)
- Material that "looks nice in photos but feels cheap in person"
- No close-up texture shots
- "See-through" or "very thin" complaints in reviews

Price behavior:
- Under $20: "Let me try, I can return it" — return rate expectation built in
- $20-60: moderate research, fit reviews critical
- $60-150: brand matters, style durability expected
- Try Before You Buy (where available) changes the risk calculus entirely
- Per-wear cost thinking for basics vs one-time occasion pieces

Review scanning habits:
- Filters by reviewer body type mentions
- "I'm 5'6, 140 lbs and the M fits perfectly" — this one review can close the sale
- Photo reviews of real people wearing the item are the ultimate converter
- Fabric quality degradation after washing is a common 3-star trigger
- Color-match photos (indoor vs outdoor lighting) carry special weight

## 7. Grocery & Pantry

Primary anxiety: freshness and taste.

What this shopper checks first:
- Expiration date mentions in reviews ("arrived with 2 months left")
- Per-unit or per-ounce price comparison
- Subscribe & Save availability and discount percentage
- Packaging integrity (shipping damage is the #1 complaint for food)
- Ingredient list and allergen information
- Whether the product is the same version as what they find in stores

Specific trust breakers:
- Reviews about receiving expired or near-expiration products
- Packaging that arrived crushed, opened, or leaking
- Price per unit that's significantly higher than local store price
- Ingredient changes ("they changed the formula")
- Artificial ingredients in a product marketed as "natural"
- Taste described as "off" or "different from usual" in recent reviews

Price behavior:
- Per-unit/per-ounce comparison is automatic and habitual
- Subscribe & Save is the default for pantry staples (5-15% off)
- Willingness to pay more for specialty/organic/imported, but only with clear differentiation
- Bulk buying creates value expectation: "If I'm buying 24 bars, I want a deal"
- Threshold awareness: is it cheaper at Costco, Target, or the local store?

Review scanning habits:
- Taste is subjective — polarized reviews are expected and normal
- Searches for specific allergen mentions
- Recent reviews dominate because formulas and packaging change
- "Not the same as it used to be" reviews create serious hesitation
- Delivery condition reports (melted chocolate, crushed chips) matter more than for other categories

## 8. Tools & Hardware

Primary anxiety: spec accuracy and real-world performance.

What this shopper checks first:
- Exact specifications (torque, voltage, material grade, dimensions)
- Whether specs match the job they need it for
- Replacement part and accessory availability
- Professional vs consumer grade distinction
- Weight and ergonomics for extended use
- Brand reputation in the trades

Specific trust breakers:
- Specifications that sound inflated or impossible for the price point
- "Made in China" for a tool claiming professional-grade performance
- Missing material grade information (stainless steel what grade? what alloy?)
- Reviews mentioning breakage under normal use
- No warranty or vague warranty terms
- Assembly or setup described as requiring tools not included
- Battery life claims that don't match review reports

Price behavior:
- Under $25: disposable mindset, acceptable if it lasts one project
- $25-75: moderate expectation, should survive regular home use
- $75-200: "buy once, cry once" territory, durability is the deciding factor
- $200+: professional-grade expectation, brand loyalty (DeWalt, Milwaukee, Makita) dominates
- Kit vs individual pricing comparison is habitual

Review scanning habits:
- Searches for specific use-case matches ("used this to drill through concrete")
- Professional tradespeople reviews carry extra authority
- "I've used [brand X] for 20 years" loyalty statements influence heavily
- Failure mode descriptions are more useful than praise
- Photo reviews showing internal components, real job sites, or wear patterns
- Weight and balance comments matter for hand tools

## Applying Category Lenses

When a product falls between categories (e.g., a kitchen scale is both Home & Kitchen and Electronics), use the category lens that matches the shopper's primary concern. If unclear, apply both and note which perspective drives the answer.

For products in categories not listed above, stay with the core shopping logic and note that no specific category lens was applied.
